Web Wide Accessibility

The internet is for everyone.

The oldest standing tenant for the world wide web is that it doesn’t matter who you are: you will be welcomed. This of course includes people who live with disabilities. Accessibility is defined by usability.gov as, “Accessibility focuses on how a disabled person accesses or benefits from a site, system or application” but accessibility goes beyond that. Creating an accessible website is largely just implementing good coding practices overall, many of which happen to benefit disabled users.

As 3pillarglobal.com puts it, “The average user gets an improved user experience, users with disabilities can now be part of your website, and as a business owner you get a site that is accessible and useful to all users so you also fulfil any legal obligations that you might have.”

Which brings up another useful point, according to the same website mentions that “As a business owner you get a site that is accessible and usable to all users so you also fulfil any legal obligations that you might have.” And that’s not some scare mongering tactic. The UN has guidelines around site accessibility. In fact, in 2000 the Sydney olympics were sued over this. So beyond good practice, it also could save you some cash.

Besides this, it’s not hard. Pablo Stanley from the collective notes that, “We found 28 violations that we needed to solve on the homepage alone… [but] We were able to get to zero errors in a couple of days.” And that’s pretty common overall. A lot of accessible design is small tweaks or more efficient coding overall that makes our job as developers much easier. In fact everything that Mr. Stanley mentions is incredibly easy to implement, and I would highly recommend his blog post as a starting point for any developer beginning to focus on accessibility. The internet is for everyone. And there are plenty of resources to help it stay that way. And a lot of those resources improve the experience for people regardless of disability. It’s not just about designing for disability. It’s about designing an internet that’s more accessible for everyone.
