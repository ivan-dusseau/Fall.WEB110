Forming A Form

On an internet saturated with forms, it’s important to think about how we can make them better.

Nearly everyone is going to come into contact with one kind of form or another on the web. But that doesn’t mean that they’re universal or well designed. A key element to drawing in clients and users is making a website that is intuitive, and this bleeds all the way down to the forms that they will need to fill out. Consider the needs of your user, and all of the different potential users your website will encounter.

Can someone older understand and fill out the form? Does it support multiple languages? Can someone with impaired vision fill it out? Can anyone decipher your error messages? I’m personally colorblind, and something that I struggled a lot with was form fields that didn’t have clear labels above them, as the grey on grey in field format being hard for my eyes to distinguish.

Consider also labeling your elements within the source code so that you might be able to use the website with a text to speech annotator. Try to avoid time limits unless absolutely necessary, and tell the user when something is correct or incorrect clearly.

A good form should tell you not only when something doesn’t go through, but also why it didn’t. Adhering to these basic practices, and more linked throughout, will make your website more usable to those with disabilities, and better for those without. Especially with something as common as forms. Make sure that your labels are clear, and readable even through a text to speech program. Make sure the colors are contrasting, and that they’re not the only indicator of success or failure. It’s important to remember to screen your forms (and whole website) to a large audience before ever publishing it to avoid lawsuits, and general confusion. The internet is for everyone, and it’s web developers’ responsibilities to help keep it that way.
